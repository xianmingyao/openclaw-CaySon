# Launch Readiness

Launch readiness is a portfolio of risks, not a vibe check. The goal is not to prove optimism. The goal is to decide whether the current plan deserves more capital and attention.

## Product Readiness

Define the MVP as the smallest product that delivers the core job reliably.

- Must-have: required to solve the job credibly.
- Nice-to-have: increases conversion, delight, or retention but is not essential to initial success.

Questions:

- Does the product solve one painful problem clearly?
- Are the core workflows stable?
- Are the known defects acceptable at launch volume?

So what: shipping more surface area than the team can support slows learning.

## Market Readiness

Look for timing signals:

- Search interest or category awareness rising.
- Customers actively stitching together bad substitutes.
- Regulation, platform shifts, or cultural changes creating new openings.
- Validation from preorder, pilot, waitlist, or paid acquisition tests.

So what: a good product can still fail if the market is not ready to pull.

## Channel Readiness

Assess whether the route to market exists today:

- D2C site performance and analytics.
- Retail relationships or marketplace readiness.
- Content, merchandising, and conversion assets.
- Fulfillment and inventory systems.

So what: channel gaps make product-market fit look weaker than it is.

## Go-To-Market Readiness

Check:

- Positioning is crisp.
- Messaging lands with target customers.
- Launch sequence is defined.
- Internal teams know the story and the plan.

So what: if the market cannot understand the product quickly, launch spend turns into noise.

## Operations Readiness

Confirm:

- Supply chain resilience.
- Support workflows.
- Returns handling.
- Spare parts or replacement plans where relevant.
- Incident response for quality failures.

So what: operational weakness is often the hidden reason promising launches implode.

## Financial Readiness

Validate:

- Gross margin at expected mix.
- Payback logic by channel.
- Break-even timing.
- Cash runway under base and downside cases.

So what: if the company cannot survive the learning phase, the launch is not ready.

## Kill Criteria

Do not launch when:

- The MVP still misses the core job.
- Demand validation is weak and the team is rationalizing it away.
- Unit economics fail even under optimistic but plausible assumptions.
- Operations cannot absorb expected failure modes.
- The company needs a perfect launch to survive.

The strongest launch decision is sometimes a delay or cancellation.
