# Market Sizing

Market sizing should produce numbers you can defend in a room with finance, investors, and operators. Start from the decision the number supports: fundraising, category entry, channel selection, or expansion timing.

## Top-Down Vs Bottom-Up

Use both whenever possible.

Top-down is useful when:

- The market is mature and externally covered.
- You need a fast directional view.
- Public company disclosures provide credible anchors.

Bottom-up is useful when:

- The market is emerging or fragmented.
- Distribution constraints matter.
- The product monetizes in nonstandard ways.

Triangulate rather than choose one method. If top-down and bottom-up differ wildly, the disagreement is the insight. So what: the sizing model is only believable when the methods roughly converge or when the reason for divergence is explained.

## Unit Economics As An Input

Sizing is not just buyers times price. Unit economics determine what part of the market is economically serviceable.

- CAC limits reachable segments.
- Gross margin limits channel choices.
- Support and returns costs shrink real profitability.
- Attachment, repeat purchase, and churn affect revenue per customer.

So what: a huge TAM with broken unit economics is not a market, it is an audience.

## Defining TAM, SAM, And SOM

- TAM: all demand that could plausibly exist for the job over time.
- SAM: the part of that demand your current product, geography, pricing, and channel can actually serve.
- SOM: the share you can capture within a realistic planning horizon.

Define SAM using real go-to-market constraints:

- Geography.
- Regulatory requirements.
- Price band.
- Channel access.
- Product readiness.
- Brand permission.

So what: SAM should feel smaller and more uncomfortable than TAM. That is a sign it is honest.

## Adjacent Market Expansion

Model expansion in stages:

1. Initial wedge.
2. Nearby adjacency with shared buyers or channel.
3. Broader platform or ecosystem expansion.

For each stage, specify:

- What capability unlocks the adjacency.
- What customer overlap exists.
- What channel or cost advantage carries over.

Do not add adjacent TAM blindly. So what: adjacency should follow strategic logic, not arithmetic appetite.

## Sources Hierarchy

Use sources in this order when building assumptions:

1. SEC filings and public company segment disclosures.
2. Credible industry reports with methodology.
3. Sell-side or analyst estimates.
4. Management claims and media interviews.

If you use weaker sources, say so. So what: source quality is part of the argument, not a footnote.

## Red Flags

- Circular logic: "We can get 1% of a billion-dollar market because it is big."
- Double-counting adjacent segments.
- Conflating TAM and SAM.
- Assuming all users pay the same price.
- Ignoring replacement cycles and frequency of purchase.
- Treating households, users, and devices as interchangeable units.

The best market sizing models are simple enough to audit and specific enough to challenge.
