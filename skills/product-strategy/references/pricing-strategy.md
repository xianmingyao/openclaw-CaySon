# Pricing Strategy

Pricing is a product decision, not a finance afterthought. The price sets expectations, filters segments, shapes channel viability, and often determines what the product must become.

## Value-Based Pricing

Start with willingness to pay, not cost-plus.

Methods:

- Van Westendorp style threshold interviews for directional bounds.
- Conjoint or forced tradeoff tests for feature-value relationships.
- Behavioral proxies: current substitute spend, time saved, or revenue unlocked.
- Landing page or preorder testing for real market response.

Estimate willingness to pay by segment, not for the whole market. So what: a blended average often hides the profitable wedge.

## Competitive Reference Pricing

Competitive prices are anchors, not instructions.

- Anchor above competitors when you offer lower perceived risk, stronger brand, or a clear outcome advantage.
- Anchor below competitors when you need fast adoption, lower switching friction, or channel trial.
- Price near the leader when you want to signal legitimacy and your product can support the comparison.

Never choose a price solely because the market leader uses it. So what: reference pricing should support positioning, not replace it.

## Price Sensitivity Indicators

Watch for:

- High substitution behavior.
- Low switching cost.
- Frequent discount response.
- Strong elasticity in conversion or retention.
- Customer language focused on deals instead of outcomes.

Low sensitivity often appears when:

- The product is tied to workflow or identity.
- Failure costs are high.
- Setup effort is painful to repeat.

So what: elasticity determines whether pricing is a growth lever or a tax on adoption.

## Bundling And Unbundling

Bundle when:

- Buyers want a complete solution.
- Cross-sell improves retention.
- A bundle hides complexity and reduces comparison shopping.

Unbundle when:

- Segments differ sharply in willingness to pay.
- Add-ons carry strong margin.
- The base product must stay accessible.

So what: bundling is strongest when it increases perceived value faster than it increases complexity.

## Price Tier Architecture

Good/better/best works when:

- Segments genuinely differ.
- The upgrade path is intuitive.
- The middle tier is the intended bestseller.

It fragments when:

- Differences are too subtle.
- Support burden rises faster than revenue.
- Sales needs custom explanation to justify tiers.

So what: tiering should simplify choice, not create internal complexity and buyer confusion.

## Launch Pricing Vs Mature Pricing

Use penetration pricing when:

- Adoption and installed base matter more than early margin.
- Network effects or ecosystem lock-in improve over time.
- Brand is unknown and trial is the main barrier.

Use skimming when:

- Demand is time-sensitive.
- Early adopters value novelty or status.
- Supply is constrained.
- Premium signaling matters.

So what: launch price is a sequencing decision. Mature price reflects the market you have earned.

## Margin Structure By Category

- Hardware: gross margin must absorb returns, warranty, channel cuts, and inventory risk. Low margin pricing leaves no room for mistakes.
- Software: high gross margin allows experimentation, but support and sales costs still shape viable ACV.
- Services: utilization and labor capacity matter more than sticker price alone.
- Consumables: starter kit economics can be weak if repeat purchase is strong, but only when retention is proven.

The pricing recommendation should always connect price, segment, and business model.
